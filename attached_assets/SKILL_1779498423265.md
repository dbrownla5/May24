# WLC Voice Recovery

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when Dayna Brown or The Well Lived Citizen needs to recover a clean, authentic content foundation from messy source material, including:

- Parsed repo batches such as `ALL_FOR_GEMINI.md` and `Dashboard-_COPY.md`
- Git repositories, app builds, site copy, readmes, prompts, docs, or exported AI conversations
- Repeated AI-generated claims that something is "LOCKED," "FINAL," "approved," or canonical when it is not
- Stale taglines, old service descriptions, outdated bios, abandoned positioning, or conflicting brand language
- Content polluted by generic AI polish, inflated certainty, duplicated drafts, or voice drift
- A handoff package for site builders, designers, copywriters, developers, or future AI agents

This skill is not for freezing creative exploration. It protects the source of truth while preserving room for brainstorming, experiments, and new strategic options.

## Usage Discipline

Before doing expensive work, use the smallest already-parsed source that can answer the question.

1. If Dayna mentions a parsed batch, digest, Gemini file, dashboard markdown, Jules output, or repo summary, inspect that first.
2. Do not launch broad repo audits, multi-agent scans, or parallel content reviews unless Dayna explicitly approves that usage.
3. If the likely source file is known, inspect only that file first, then report what it contains and what remains unresolved.
4. Prefer targeted source review over re-processing repositories. The skill exists to reduce repeated work, not recreate it.
5. When asking for confirmation, be specific: name the file, repo, or batch to inspect and the expected next output.

## Core Purpose

The goal is to separate signal from residue:

- **Signal**: durable language, strategic intent, authentic voice, accurate business facts, useful frameworks, and content that still reflects The Well Lived Citizen.
- **Residue**: AI filler, false finality, stale positioning, repeated hallucinated taglines, over-polished generic copy, outdated information, and drafts that became sticky only because they were repeated.

The final output should make it easy to rebuild from a trustworthy foundation without forcing Dayna to keep re-explaining the same context.

## Voice North Star

When evaluating or rewriting content, preserve this working voice profile unless Dayna provides newer guidance:

- **Strategic but human**: shows executive-level acumen without becoming corporate sludge.
- **Direct and useful**: gets to the point, names the real issue, and avoids filler.
- **Warm without being soft**: cares about people and systems without sounding precious or vague.
- **Sharp but not performative**: confident, witty when appropriate, never trying too hard.
- **Operationally grounded**: connects ideas to behavior, systems, handoffs, decisions, and outcomes.
- **Story-aware but not sentimental**: respects the emotional layer of homes, archives, objects, loss, and transitions without turning the brand into soft-focus therapy language.
- **Builder-minded**: understands that Dayna needs practical systems, usable handoffs, and clean execution, not endless brand contemplation.

Avoid copy that sounds like:

- A generic AI brand strategist
- A wellness lifestyle influencer
- A venture-backed SaaS landing page with no soul
- A consultant hiding behind jargon
- A motivational poster dressed up as strategy
- A generic luxury reseller targeting status anxiety
- An elder-care, estate-sale, or legal-planning service

## Non-Negotiable Guardrails

### Do not treat repetition as truth

If a phrase appears many times, verify whether it is actually approved, current, and useful. AI systems often amplify old drafts by repeating them.

### Do not use false finality

Avoid declaring language "LOCKED," "FINAL," "canonical," "approved," or "source of truth" unless Dayna explicitly says it is. If a status is uncertain, label it as uncertain.

If source material itself uses `[LOCKED]`, treat it as a claim requiring provenance, not automatic truth. Some parsed material shows `[LOCKED]` language beside stale or conflicting instructions, so verify with the source hierarchy and current Dayna direction.

### Do not flatten voice into generic polish

Do not replace specific, textured, founder-level language with vague claims like "empowering individuals," "transformational experiences," "holistic solutions," or "unlock your potential" unless those phrases are explicitly approved.

### Do not delete useful tension

The Well Lived Citizen may combine strategy, operations, humanity, taste, systems, business development, and lived experience. Do not force it into one narrow category just because AI prefers clean buckets.

### Keep brainstorming separate from foundation

Exploration is allowed. The mistake is letting exploratory copy masquerade as approved brand truth. Clearly label brainstorming sections as such.

### Protect creative flexibility

Do not aggressively overwrite, rename, or hard-freeze Dayna's ideas when she is testing a promo, phrase, service angle, or copy rhythm. The skill should separate brainstorms from canon, not punish brainstorming.

## Source Classification System

When reviewing files, docs, repos, builds, or copy dumps, classify each useful item into one of these buckets.

| Classification | Meaning | Treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Canonical | Explicitly approved by Dayna or clearly current source of truth | Preserve, cite location, and use as foundation |
| Strong signal | Feels authentic and strategically useful but needs confirmation | Keep, label as promising, and ask for approval if needed |
| Useful fragment | Contains a phrase, concept, structure, or insight worth saving | Extract the useful part; do not preserve the whole draft |
| Brainstorming | Exploratory idea, variant, or possible direction | Keep separate from approved language |
| Stale | Previously useful but likely outdated, contradicted, or superseded | Archive with reason; do not reuse by default |
| AI residue | Generic, repetitive, falsely final, inflated, or contaminated by AI drift | Remove from foundation; optionally document pattern |
| Conflict | Two or more versions disagree on facts, positioning, offers, or tone | Flag as a decision needed |

## Workflow

### Intake the material

Start by identifying the source set and what each source likely represents:

1. Start with already-parsed files if present, especially `ALL_FOR_GEMINI.md` and `Dashboard-_COPY.md`.
2. List repos, folders, files, builds, docs, transcripts, or content dumps reviewed.
3. Note timestamps, file names, commit context, page URLs, or other provenance.
4. Identify likely source types: production copy, draft copy, AI conversation, documentation, placeholder content, config, readme, build artifact, or archived material.
5. Separate live/public-facing content from drafts and experimental material.
6. If a raw repo audit would be useful, pause and ask Dayna before launching it.

### Detect AI drift and residue

Scan for these contamination signals:

- Repeated status terms: "LOCKED," "FINAL," "approved," "source of truth," "canonical," "definitive"
- Generic AI phrasing: "seamlessly," "empower," "elevate," "unlock," "transform," "holistic," "curated experiences," "bespoke solutions"
- Overconfident summary language without evidence
- Multiple near-identical taglines or bios with tiny variations
- Content that sounds polished but says nothing concrete
- Contradictory business descriptions repeated across drafts
- Placeholder claims that look finished because they appear in build files
- Old positioning resurfacing as if current
- AI apologies and reset declarations that should not become brand copy, such as "I apologize for drifting" or "I stripped out all the old assumptions"
- Tooling promises that sound emotionally satisfying but are not verified, such as "I am building your reality"
- Instructions that were meant for an app agent but are accidentally treated as public-facing site copy

Do not remove a phrase solely because it is common. Remove or downgrade it when it fails the authenticity, specificity, or provenance test.

### Extract the durable foundation

Build a clean foundation from the surviving signal:

1. Identify confirmed business facts: company name, audience, services, offers, geography, founder bio details, and current operating model.
2. Extract durable strategic themes: what The Well Lived Citizen actually helps people or businesses do.
3. Capture voice patterns that feel like Dayna: cadence, sentence length, humor, directness, emotional register, and preferred framing.
4. Preserve strong phrases, but do not overfit the brand around a single tagline.
5. Identify terms to prefer, terms to avoid, and terms requiring approval.
6. Separate internal AI/dashboard instructions from client-facing website copy.
7. Separate emotional origin-story language from service-page conversion copy.

### Resolve or flag conflicts

When sources disagree, do not silently choose the version that appears most often. Make a decision table:

| Issue | Version A | Version B | Evidence | Recommendation | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Use these statuses:

- **Resolved**: supported by clear evidence or explicit direction
- **Recommended**: best available choice, still needs Dayna approval
- **Blocked**: cannot decide without Dayna
- **Archived**: old version retained only for historical context

Common WLC conflicts to check:

- Business name: The Well Lived Citizen vs The Well Lived Citizen Co.
- Tagline: current, retired, page-specific, or brainstorm.
- Service labels: House Calls vs Special Delivery; legacy planning vs estate-related language.
- Pricing: $150/hr references vs $175/hr references; flex blocks; quick books; large-project quotes.
- Resale split: orientation of the 55/45 split and whether it is net after fees.
- Domain and parent-site redirects.
- Whether a source is an internal dashboard rule, public website copy, or developer handoff instruction.

### Produce the clean handoff

The final handoff should be structured enough that another agent, developer, copywriter, or designer can use it without reintroducing drift.

Include:

1. **Executive summary**: what was cleaned, what changed, and what is now safe to build from.
2. **Source inventory**: what was reviewed and what each source was used for.
3. **Canonical foundation**: approved or highest-confidence facts, positioning, voice rules, and current service language.
4. **Strong signal bank**: promising phrases and concepts that are not yet final.
5. **Archived residue**: stale, generic, or contaminated content patterns to avoid.
6. **Decision log**: unresolved conflicts and recommended choices.
7. **Implementation notes**: where the clean language should be applied in site pages, repos, docs, metadata, or future prompts.
8. **Usage note**: whether more source inspection is needed and what Dayna must approve before it happens.

## Review Checklist

Before presenting a cleaned foundation, verify:

- Existing parsed files were inspected before raw repo audits were proposed.
- The output distinguishes approved language from recommendations and brainstorms.
- No repeated AI draft is treated as canonical just because it appeared often.
- Stale taglines and outdated bios are archived, not silently reused.
- The language sounds like a strategic operator with taste, not a generic consultant.
- Every unresolved conflict is visible in a decision log.
- The handoff gives future builders enough context to avoid restarting the same cleanup loop.
- Any recommendation to inspect more repos or launch agents is explicit and requires Dayna approval.

## How to Respond to Dayna

Use direct, structured updates. Do not patronize, over-explain, or suggest breaks. When something is messy, say what is messy and what you are doing about it.

Good response pattern:

> I found three layers here: current signal, stale positioning, and AI residue that got repeated until it looked official. I’m separating those now so the handoff can preserve the real foundation without treating old drafts as law.

Avoid:

> Great news, your brand voice is now fully locked and finalized!

Also avoid launching broad repo work when Dayna has already pointed to a parsed batch. Say:

> I’ll start with the parsed batch first. If it doesn’t answer the question, I’ll tell you exactly what is missing before touching any raw repo.

## Escalation Questions

Ask Dayna only when a decision materially changes the foundation. Good questions include:

- "Which audience is primary right now: individual clients, business clients, or both?"
- "Is this phrase actually approved, or is it just familiar because it kept showing up?"
- "Should this service remain public-facing, or is it internal language for now?"
- "Do you want this preserved as a brainstorm, or promoted into the working foundation?"
- "Do you want me to inspect the raw repo, or stay inside the parsed batch?"

Do not ask Dayna to restate context that can be inferred from the source material. The point of this skill is to reduce repetition, not create more of it.
